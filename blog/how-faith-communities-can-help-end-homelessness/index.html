How Faith Communities Can Help End Homelessness

By Kevin Nye · National Faith Coalition to End Homelessness

Starting to drive at sixteen was the first time I was aware of how noticeably uncomfortable I was ignoring someone asking for change at a stoplight. I didn’t know if I should look the person in the eye, pretend not to see them, or duck out of sight. After a few months, I asked my pastor how I should respond, but his response didn’t resonate. Silver and gold have I not, he said, echoing Peter in Acts. But silver and gold I did have. That was the whole problem.

A close friend and I decided we would at least try to mitigate some of the suffering. We would take turns buying Jack-in-the-Box tacos and $5 pizzas, and head to the overpass where my neighbors experiencing homelessness were often trying to find respite. We did little to actually help, but we became close friends with quite a few people we spent time with over a couple years. I didn’t have a theology yet. I had a nudge, some of my mom’s change in my pocket, and a stubborn sense that it shouldn’t be normal seeing people struggle to survive.

That nudge would never go away. But it took years — of formal theology, of hard lessons at the margins of broken systems, of sitting with people who taught me more than any book could — before I understood the question I was actually trying to answer. Not just how to help. How to help in a way that actually helps.

In college, studying theology, I was immersed in the prophets and the psalms through practices designed to cultivate not just knowledge but a kind of seeing. Theologian Nicholas Wolterstorff helped give language to what I was beginning to feel: that the heart of God is bound up with what he calls the “quartet of the vulnerable” — widows, orphans, immigrants, and the poor. And Abraham Joshua Heschel, writing about the Hebrew prophets, said something I’ve thought about for years: if a societal system doesn’t work for one person, it is worth revolutionizing. That’s not a counsel of despair. That’s a call to think structurally about what actually needs to change.

Every faith community wrestling with homelessness in their neighborhood is ultimately asking a version of the same question this post tries to answer: how can faith communities help END homelessness — not just manage it, not just ease its symptoms, but actually end it?

The Scope of What We’re Facing

For most of the last decade, homelessness in America moved in one direction: up. So the most recent count is worth pausing on. In January 2025, the federal point-in-time count found that homelessness had fallen for the first time since 2016 — a national decline of more than three percent. It is a small number carrying a large meaning: the trend is not fixed. Homelessness responds to what we actually do about it.

What produced that decline is not a mystery. It came from the approaches the evidence has pointed to for years — getting people into stable housing first and building the services they need around them, backed by targeted resources like emergency housing vouchers. Where communities funded that work, the numbers moved. And here is the part that should sober us: those same approaches are now being defunded and dismissed in policy, even as the data shows them working. Progress this fragile can be lost as quickly as it was won.

The underlying pressures have not gone away either. The causes of homelessness remain largely structural: a housing supply that hasn’t kept pace with demand, wages that have stalled while rents have climbed, and a safety net with enough gaps to swallow people who are one crisis away from the street. Homelessness still rose in more than half the states last year. The national decline is real, but it is uneven, and it is not guaranteed to continue.

Faith communities know this. They feel it. Every major religious tradition in America carries within it a framework for responding to poverty, displacement, and suffering — and most congregations are already trying to do something. In Christianity, the entire arc of scripture is oriented toward the vulnerable. The Mosaic law structured society around the poor. The prophets raged on their behalf. Jesus identified so completely with the marginalized that Matthew 25 isn’t metaphor — it’s instruction. To encounter the person experiencing homelessness on the street is, in some theological sense, to encounter Christ himself.

Homelessness is not a peripheral issue for The Church. It is a defining one. Which means the question of how faith communities respond isn’t just practical — it’s theological. And the answer matters more than most of us have allowed ourselves to believe.

What Serves the Neighbor

The proven approaches that just produced the first national drop in homelessness since 2016 are now being dismantled in policy, on the claim that they don’t work. Faith communities will be pulled into that argument, and we should think carefully about how we enter it.

Here is something the Church can offer this moment that little else in our politics can. We do not have to care who proposed a policy, or which side claims it, or whom it helps politically. We have one question, and it is older than any administration: does this serve the good of our neighbor? When the answer is yes, we support it, whoever signed it. When the answer is no, we say so, whoever signed it. The neighbor on the street belongs to no party. Neither does our obligation to them.

What I Saw at the Shelter

When I was twenty-nine, the nudge that started at a stoplight finally pulled me somewhere specific. I took an entry-level job at an emergency shelter. I went in expecting to help. What I got was an education in something I didn’t have language for yet.

What I saw at that shelter is that I had walked into a system that had already drawn its lines, and the people inside it had been sorted before they arrived. There were the deserving and the noncompliant. The motivated and the chronic. The ones working their plan and the ones we had run out of patience for. None of these were labels anyone wrote on the wall. They didn’t have to be. The system carried them. Decisions got made through them. And most of us didn’t notice we were reading from a script that had been handed to us long before we showed up for our shift.

I want to be careful here, because I don’t think the people running that shelter were cruel. The categories came from a model the wider culture has been rehearsing for generations — one that assumes people must earn their way to stability. That’s a failure of model, not of motive. But it is still a failure. And it is still a question we should refuse to ask of our neighbors, dressed in operational language.

How Faith Communities Can Get Involved — As Individuals and Congregations

All of this asks for a response that is as serious as the problem. Here is what that looks like on the ground, for individuals and for congregations.

Learn the real story. Not the narrative you absorbed growing up — the actual data about what drives homelessness in your community. Who is experiencing it and why? What resources exist locally? What is working and what isn’t? Read. Ask questions. Attend a community forum. Talk to the people doing this work in your city. The homelessness response systems in most communities have learned things over decades that most congregations haven’t been exposed to. That knowledge should inform how we engage.

Practice seeing before you practice serving. Programs come later. Relationship is the foundation everything else gets built on. That means proximity. It means learning names. It means returning. It means refusing the label the system has handed you and looking at the person it was meant to obscure.

Support organizations already doing evidence-based work. In most communities, there are nonprofits, service providers, and housing organizations that have spent years learning what actually moves people from homelessness to stability. Find them. Give to them. Volunteer in ways they actually need. If you’re not sure where to start, NFCEH can help you identify who’s doing strong work in your area. Joining the work already happening is almost always more effective than launching something new.

Congregationally: designate someone to own this. Identify a person or a small team whose job it is to understand homelessness response in your community, build relationships with local organizations, and help the congregation engage thoughtfully. Not a committee that meets quarterly. Someone who actually learns the landscape, makes connections, and becomes a bridge.

Advocate. Zoning decisions, affordable housing bond measures, landlord-tenant policy, shelter funding — organized faith communities can move these needles in ways other stakeholders often can’t. Elected officials pay attention to congregations. The prophets weren’t just caring for individuals in need; they were confronting the systems that created need in the first place. Advocacy isn’t separate from ministry. For much of the scriptural tradition, it was the center of it.

Observe Homelessness Sunday. On October 11, 2026, faith communities across the country will pause to acknowledge the reality of homelessness in their neighborhoods, learn together, and make a collective commitment to engage. If your congregation hasn’t marked this on the calendar, it’s a concrete place to start. (Learn more about Homelessness Sunday.)

The National Faith Coalition to End Homelessness exists to make this kind of engagement possible at scale. We bring churches and faith-based organizations into shared learning, shared work, and shared advocacy — guided by best practices — so that the next time someone in your community ends up on the street, they meet a body that is awake, equipped, and coordinated.

The kid sitting at that stoplight at sixteen didn’t understand systems. He understood that something was wrong and that it demanded a response. I still believe that. What I have learned since is that the response needs to be as serious as the problem.

So — how can faith communities help END homelessness? Not alone, and not all at once. But together, connected to the work already happening, guided by the evidence, and refusing to look away.
