Love Thy Unhoused Neighbor

Understanding Homelessness

Compassion guided by wisdom starts here: what homelessness actually is, what causes it, what works to end it, and how the response system in your community fits together.

01

The real story

In January 2024, the federal point-in-time count found 771,480 people experiencing homelessness in America — and a year later, the 2025 count recorded the first national decline since 2016. That decline is worth pausing on: it means the trend is not fixed. Homelessness responds to what communities actually do about it.

Behind every number is a neighbor — someone displaced by a rent increase, a medical crisis, a lost job, a family rupture. Understanding the scale matters. Refusing to let the scale obscure the person matters more.

02

What causes it

The reality is more complicated than the narratives most of us absorbed. The causes of homelessness are largely structural: a housing supply that hasn’t kept pace with demand, wages that stalled while rents climbed, and a safety net with enough gaps to swallow people who are one crisis away from the street. Personal struggles — illness, addiction, trauma — are real, but they are far more often consequences and accelerants than root causes. Plenty of people face all three and stay housed, because housing they can afford exists for them.

03

What actually works

Two decades of evidence point the same direction: people exit homelessness when they get housing they can afford, with support built around them — not when they are asked to earn their way to stability first. This is the insight behind Housing First, the evidence-based approach that prioritizes permanent housing without preconditions. Where communities have funded this work, the numbers have moved.

The goal the field works toward is a system where homelessness is rare, brief, and non-recurring — rare because prevention catches people before they lose housing, brief because the path back indoors is fast, and non-recurring because the support holds.

04

Where your church fits

Every region already has a homelessness response system. The faithful move is not to replace it — it’s to partner with it. Churches bring what systems chronically lack: people who stay, space in the right neighborhoods, money that isn’t grant-restricted, and a moral voice that elected officials hear. The Church’s Role walks through what that partnership looks like.

Know the language of the work

These are the terms you’ll hear from the organizations in your community. Knowing them is how a congregation shows up as a partner, not a tourist.

Continuum of Care (CoC)

The coordinated local planning body managing the homelessness response in a geographic area. Every region has one — it’s the front door to the work already happening near you.

Coordinated Entry

The standardized system for assessing and prioritizing people experiencing homelessness for housing and services — so help goes where the need is greatest, not to whoever found the right door first.

Housing First

The evidence-based approach that prioritizes permanent housing without preconditions, then builds services around stability instead of making stability a prerequisite.

Permanent Supportive Housing

Long-term housing combined with supportive services, for people with complex needs who require more than a rental subsidy to stay housed.

Rapid Rehousing

Short-to-medium-term rental assistance and services that help people move out of homelessness quickly — often the right tool when a crisis, not a chronic condition, caused the loss of housing.

Landlord Engagement

Recruiting and supporting private landlords as partners in housing placements. One of the most practical gaps a congregation’s relationships can fill.

Rental Assistance

Financial support that enables people to afford and maintain housing — among the most cost-effective interventions in the entire system.

Rare, Brief, Non-Recurring

The framework goal of the field: homelessness should become a rare event, a brief experience, and one that does not repeat.
