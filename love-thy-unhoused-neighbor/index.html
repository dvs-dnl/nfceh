Love Thy Unhoused Neighbor

The Church’s Role in Ending Homelessness

Compassion guided by wisdom. The Church has always been called to the neighbor in need — the question is how to answer that call in a way that actually helps.

The Call Is Not New

Faith communities have been responding to poverty and displacement for as long as they have existed. The meal trains, the coat closets, the cots in the fellowship hall — none of this is new, and none of it is wasted. Congregations across the country are already showing up for their unhoused neighbors, often quietly, often for decades.

What’s newer is the understanding of what it takes to actually end homelessness rather than ease it. Over the past twenty years, communities have learned a great deal about what moves people from the street into lasting stability — and most congregations have never been handed that knowledge.

The Gap Between Compassion and Coordination

The reality is more complicated than most expect. The church is rich in compassion but often poor in coordination. A congregation can serve meals every week for years without ever connecting to the local housing system that could move its guests indoors permanently. The problem isn’t a lack of love. It’s a lack of connection to the work already happening.

Every region in the country has a homelessness response system — a network of providers, outreach teams, and housing programs coordinated through a local Continuum of Care. These systems know who in your community is experiencing homelessness, what they need, and where the gaps are. What they often lack is exactly what churches have in abundance: people, presence, space, and a moral voice.

What Churches Bring That Nothing Else Can

When faith communities connect their compassion to evidence-based work, the impact multiplies. Churches can offer volunteers who stay. Buildings and land in neighborhoods where housing is scarce. Donors who give faithfully. Relationships that don’t expire when a program ends. And a voice that elected officials listen to when housing decisions get made.

One of the things we’ve learned: joining the work already happening is almost always more effective than launching something new.
